Launching Summer 2022 in New Orleans

Not in Los Angeles, not in New York City, not in London or Paris or Prague. Right here in New Orleans, the most innovative concept in fashion, at the intersection of web3 and art, is coming to life this summer. And we invite you to be part of the launch by giving us a follow us on instagram for the latest announcements. 

We've all been doing this wrong.

We buy art from talented artists and hang it up in private places, while every day we promote clothing brands (that really don't need free advertising) on our bodies. 

We love art, but we cut the artists right out of the best business opportunity imaginable Why is it that the best contemporary artists are not featured in the best clothing brands?

Stitched.eth uses propietary web3 technology to create an ecosystem where clothing brands, retailers and art collectors can trade copyright-protected designs of artists, an ecosystem where there is real equity for artists, and where all of us can wear the artist as a brand. In the new world that we're creating, we will wear the artist, not the clothing brand. 

We've started with "street art" but we'll be adding artists of all stripes. As soon as your favorite artist joins our ecosystem, we can then help connect them with an established manufacturer, an up-and-coming designer, a retailer, or a collector that wants to invest in the artist. 

Project Summary and FAQs

The stitched.eth ecosystem aims to solve a host of problems that have plagued the modern fashion industry as well as problems that have always existed for artists and art patrons. The ecosystem creates a financial interdependency between artists and art patrons, brings transparency to the fashion industry and helps to re-allocate profits away from institutional brands to the artists and designers themselves.

What's in it for Artists?

We help serious visual artists fund their projects and grow their practice by empowering them to enter the apparel market without giving up control over their designs and artwork. Artists maximize the value of their work by capturing recurring revenue through a profit-sharing model.

Art comes to life, not just through a sculpture, framed wall art, or a digital NFT, but here also through apparel - transforming artwork into utilitarian consumer products and artists into their own brands. Given the opportunity, we believe consumers would rather be a walking billboard for the artists that they love than for the powerful, damaging brands that they are forced to choose among to clothe themselves. 

We do this by building a blockchain-based ecosystem where the authenticity of physical garments is guaranteed and copyright licenses can be transferred and utilized transparently and efficiently through copyright-embedded NFTs. 

If you're an artist, your most serious collectors and supporters want to invest in you and tell everyone about their investment in you and how much they believe in you. In the past, they could do this only by purchasing and displaying your art at whatever particular private location on earth they chose to place it. Speculators might buy your work, hoping the value goes up if you die. And in any case, you only make money at the time of the initial sale.

For artists in our stitched.eth ecosystem, your collectors can invest in NFTs that have the capability of bringing your art to the streets, and you get paid for it every time this happens. You can get more, and more deeply-connected collectors, collectors can earn a real return on their investment in your art, and consumers get accessible art that they can showcase on their bodies every day.

What's in it for Patrons, Collectors and Retailers?

We enable art patrons and collectors (and even the artists themselves) to become retailers in this new fashion industry, and in the process, economically incentivizing investment in artists in a way that has never happened before.

As a collector, you want to help the artists you invest in reach their full potential. In the past, the only way to do this was by purchasing the artist's work and maybe donating some additional money, time or resources. There's nothing wrong with that, but it does create some problems. 

If you're a serious collector, you end up purchasing more artwork than you could ever display, and so much of it ends up in storage or else you have to dedicate significant resources to finding homes for your acquisitions. 

Further, there's no direct, recognizable economic benefit to investing into the artist beyond purchasing their art. Throughout history, we have just accepted this as the "way it is" - that collectors could become patrons, but only at their own expense and for the benefit of artists (which also creates a problematic power dynamic) - but it doesn't have to be this way. We have created a mechanism to align the incentives - to create economic interdependency in an ecosystem that economically benefits both the artists and patrons. Human nature being what it is, when it makes economic sense for people, people will be more willing to support artists than they would be if you rely solely on the kindness of their hearts.

The second problem with the old model is that if the artist that you believed in becomes famous and widely collected, the only way for you to realize a financial gain was to sell the work you purchased - but as a collector, you want to enjoy the art, not sell it later. But even selling it is not easy. As everyone knows, art resale markets are dominated by auction houses that only want to deal with an exclusive subset of well-known artists. It's often very difficult to resell outside of this context and nearly impossible to authenticate art that you might want to sell.

So what's all this have to do with retailers and the fashion industry?

Here's where things get interesting.

We have developed a very interesting way to deploy blockchain technology and copyright law in combination with the fashion and art worlds. 

International copyright law gives artists the inherent power over reproduction of their own artwork, including the power to license it. NFTs give blockchains the power to transparently record and identify owners of digital assets. So, by digitizing copyright licenses conferred by artists, we can accurately identify the owners of the copyright licenses in a trustless and frictionless public marketplace. We then empower the NFT hodlers to place orders for apparel featuring their specific and exclusive copyright-licensed design directly through the artist.

In the stitched.eth ecosystem, artists transform their work into copyright-embedded NFTs, and all you have to do is purchase the NFT in order to get the copyright license, seamlessly transforming collectors into retailers. 

The artist captures profit through the production of the garments, and the NFT hodler can then keep or resell the garments as they wish. The garments are all tracked on the blockchain with a digital certificate of authenticity that cannot be replicated. And all of this is done transparently so that anyone in the world can recognize and prove that any particular garment is the real deal. 

Accordingly, NFT hodlers are economically incentivized to promote the artist's designs - the value of both the NFT and the garments increases as the artist's profile increases. Collectors who own NFTs can start their own e-commerce or real, physical retail stores to drop their exclusive products - all while maintaining their initial investment in the NFT.

How does it all work?

It all begins with the artist. 

An artist creates an iconic design or work that can be translated onto apparel. That might be a stencil, a graphic image for screen printing, an applique ...or whatever else the artist can imagine.

The artist licenses the exclusive right to reproduce this design on apparel to stitched.eth. By virtue of doing this, the artist does lose the right to reproduce that piece on any other clothing, but the artist maintains the rights (1) to use the design on any other medium, (2) to refuse to produce the design for any particular licensee, and (3) to use any other designs on apparel that haven't also been licensed. 

stitched.eth then sub-licenses this exclusive right among a series of NFTs. The specific number of NFTs may change, but right now the number is 15 (to be clear, once the series is minted, no more can be added to that NFT series - the specific number of NFTs in each series will be established and permanently fixed at the time of minting). Therefore, no one else in the world can reproduce the specific design on clothing except for the holders of those 15 minted NFTs. The sub-license further restricts the NFT-holder's reproduction rights by: (1) limiting the number of reproductions per year - usually 100 per year - except that the number of reproductions can accumulate over the years (these are the "design allocations") and (2) restricting the person who can actually manufacture the reproductions to the artist who created the design. 

Accordingly, the artist can say "no" to any particular design request, but the artist cannot do any reproductions of the work without the approval of an NFT holder. The NFT holder must then negotiate directly with the artist to get the design manufactured. In the event that the artist and the NFT holder cannot agree on a reproduction run, then the yearly allocation goes unused, continues to accumulate with the NFT, and the NFT holder may choose to retain the NFT or sell it to someone else who can then use the allocation. 

Whenever the artist accepts an order (and gets paid for it, at prices determined by the artist), then the artist produces the reproduction and delivers it to the NFT holder. stitched.eth deducts the design allocations from the NFT by updating the corresponding NFT's webpage (the location of which is embedded in its metadata) and registers each reproduction by minting an NFT. The physical goods must be conferred together with the NFTs in order for them to be considered authentic. 

stitched.eth maintains the NFT addresses for all the world to see right here on this decentralized website. All of the updates are hashed on IPFS, so even if every core dev dies off in a plane crash, the IPFS hash will remain for anyone else to take over the project or confirm a garment's authenticity. 

What blockchain do you use for the NFTs?

We use two different blockchains for the NFTs. The design NFTs that confer the copyright license are built on the ethereum blockchain. The garment NFTs are built on the Stacks blockchain.
